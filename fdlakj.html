Kinematics Forces Energy Waves Electricity Problems & Solutions

Kinematics

Kinematics deals with the motion of objects without considering the forces causing the motion. We study quantities like displacement, velocity, and acceleration, and their relationships using calculus.

Forces

Understand Newton's laws of motion and how forces affect the movement of objects. Use vector calculus to break down forces and solve for acceleration or equilibrium.

Energy and Work

Learn about kinetic and potential energy, and how energy is conserved. Calculus allows us to understand work as an integral of force over distance, giving deep insights into energy transformations.

Waves

Explore how waves transfer energy through different media, including sound and light waves. Derivatives and integrals are used to understand wave speed, frequency, and wavelength.

Electricity and Magnetism

Dive into electric fields, potentials, and currents. Calculus helps derive formulas for electric circuits and understand how fields interact with charges.

Problem 1.1: Applications on Integration

Consider a graph of \( \sin x \) between 0 and \( \pi \). If the graph is revolved around the x-axis, what is the ratio between the moment of inertia around the y-axis and the mass of the surface (the graph is hollow, which means its mass is the mass of the surface only, also the surface is uniform).

Solution:

For a solid of revolution generated around the x-axis, the moment of inertia are as follows:

\[ I_X = rac{\pi ho}{2} \int_{x_0}^{x_f} f(x)^4 \, dx \] \[ I_Y = rac{I_X}{2} + \pi ho \int_{x_0}^{x_f} x^2 f(x)^2 \, dx \]

Where \( ho \) is the density of the shape, \( f(x) \) is the function, \( I_X \) is the moment of inertia around the x-axis, and \( I_Y \) is the moment of inertia around the y-axis.

Combining these two equations yields:

\[ I_Y = rac{\pi ho}{4} \int_{x_0}^{x_f} f(x)^4 \, dx + \pi ho \int_{x_0}^{x_f} x^2 f(x)^2 \, dx = \pi ho \left[ rac{1}{4} \int_{x_0}^{x_f} f(x)^4 \, dx + \int_{x_0}^{x_f} x^2 f(x)^2 \, dx ight] \]

Now, we have to find the mass. Notice that the graph is hollow, which means that the mass is just that of the surface. Also, as it's uniform, it has a constant density. Then:

\[ ho = rac{m}{V} \implies m = ho V \]

Substituting the value of \( V \) as:

\[ V = \pi \int_{x_0}^{x_f} f(x)^2 \, dx \]

Then, the mass of the shape is:

\[ m = \pi ho \int_{x_0}^{x_f} f(x)^2 \, dx \]

Now, we just have to find the ratio between the moment of inertia and the mass:

\[ rac{I_Y}{m} = rac{\pi ho \left[ rac{1}{4} \int_{x_0}^{x_f} f(x)^4 \, dx + \int_{x_0}^{x_f} x^2 f(x)^2 \, dx ight]}{\pi ho \int_{x_0}^{x_f} f(x)^2 \, dx} \]

Simplifying the expression and substituting with \( f(x) = \sin x \):

\[ rac{I_Y}{m} = rac{ rac{1}{4} \int_{0}^{\pi} \sin^4 x \, dx + \int_{0}^{\pi} x^2 \sin^2 x \, dx}{\int_{0}^{\pi} \sin^2 x \, dx} \approx 2.977368134 \]

Hence, the ratio between the moment of inertia around the y-axis and the mass is: \( I_Y : m = 2.977:1 \approx 3:1 \).

Problem 1.2: Volume of a Revolved Bottle

Mazen wants to make a new top cola bottle with a bigger volume, so he chooses the design of the bottle to be \( rac{1}{1+x} \) revolved around the y-axis from 0 to 4. What is the volume of the bottle?

Solution:

To evaluate this problem, we can easily use the formula for the volume of rotation around the y-axis:

\[ V = \pi \int_a^b r(y)^2 \, dy \]

However, we have to first find both the function and the boundaries in terms of \( y \).

\[ y = rac{1}{1+x} \\ rac{1}{y} = x + 1 \\ x = rac{1-y}{y} \]

For the boundaries:

\[ ext{Lower boundary: } y = rac{1}{1+0} = 1 \\ ext{Upper boundary: } y = rac{1}{1+4} = rac{1}{5} \]

Since the lower boundary now is larger than the upper one, we will have to reverse them when evaluating the integral to get a positive volume. Now, evaluating the integral:

\[ V = \pi \int_{ rac{1}{5}}^1 \left( rac{1-y}{y} ight)^2 \, dy = \pi \int_{ rac{1}{5}}^1 \left( 1 - rac{2}{y} + rac{1}{y^2} ight) \, dy \]

The first and third terms are easy to work with, just the second one involves a substitution:

\[ \int rac{2}{y} \, dy = 2 \ln |y| + C \]

Then, the integral ends up as:

\[ V = \pi \left[ y - 2 \ln |y| - rac{1}{y} ight]_{ rac{1}{5}}^1 \approx 1.58112 \pi \]

Hence, the volume between 0 and 4 is: \( V \approx 1.58 \pi \approx 4.963 \).

Problem 1.3: Surface Area and Area Under Curve

If the function \( y = e^x \) is rotated around the x-axis, find the ratio between its surface area from 1 to 6 and the area under the curve of \( y \) from 0 to 5.

Solution:

First, find the area under the curve of \( e^x \):

\[ A = \int_0^5 e^x \, dx = e^5 - 1 \]

Second, find the surface area of the rotated curve about the x-axis using the surface area formula:

\[ S = 2\pi \int_1^6 e^x \sqrt{1+e^{2x}} \, dx \]

Evaluating this integral gives approximately 511301.7002. Now, find the ratio:

\[ rac{S}{A} = rac{511301.7002}{e^5 - 1} \approx 3468.49 \]

Hence, the ratio is: \( oxed{3468.49} \).

Problem 1.4: Average Acceleration

If a force \( f(x) = \sin^3 x \) is applied on Mazen, find the average acceleration between 0 and \( rac{\pi}{2} \) if Mazen has a mass of 70 kg.

Solution:

From Newton's second law, we know that force is equal to \( ma \), where \( m \) is the mass and \( a \) is the acceleration. Then:

\[ a = rac{F}{m} \implies ar{a} = rac{ar{F}}{m} \]

We can use the formula for the average force:

\[ ar{F} = rac{1}{b-a} \int_a^b F(x) \, dx \]

Substituting the values:

\[ ar{F} = rac{1}{ rac{\pi}{2} - 0} \int_0^{ rac{\pi}{2}} \sin^3 x \, dx \]

Using the formula for the integration of sines:

\[ \int \sin^n (x) \, dx = - rac{\sin^{n-1} (x) \cos (x)}{n} + rac{n-1}{n} \int \sin^{n-2} (x) \, dx \]

Applying this formula gives:

\[ \int \sin^3 x = - rac{\sin^2 x \cos x}{3} - rac{2}{3} \cos x \]

Evaluating the boundaries:

\[ \int_0^{ rac{\pi}{2}} \sin^3 x = \left[ - rac{\sin^2( rac{\pi}{2}) \cos( rac{\pi}{2})}{3} - rac{2}{3} \cos( rac{\pi}{2}) ight] - \left[ - rac{\sin^2(0) \cos(0)}{3} - rac{2}{3} \cos(0) ight] \]

This evaluates to:

\[ 0 - \left( - rac{2}{3} ight) = rac{2}{3} \]

Then:

\[ ar{F} = rac{2}{\pi} \cdot rac{2}{3} = rac{4}{3\pi} \]

Substituting this in the formula for the average acceleration:

\[ ar{a} = rac{ar{F}}{m} = rac{ rac{4}{3\pi}}{70} = rac{2}{105\pi} \]

Hence, the average acceleration is: \( ar{a} \approx 6.063 imes 10^{-3} \).

Problem 1.5: Moment of Inertia of a Plant

Mazen saw a strange plant with the shape of \( f(x) = e^x \) revolved around the x-axis from 1 to 4. The plant's density increases with distance from the x-axis where \( ho = r \), where \( r \) is the distance from the x-axis. What is the moment of inertia of the plant around the y-axis?

Solution:

To solve this problem, recall the formula for the moment of inertia around the y-axis used in Problem 1.1:

\[ I_Y = \pi \int_{x_0}^{x_f} x \, dx \left[ rac{1}{4} \int_{x_0}^{x_f} f(x)^4 \, dx + \int_{x_0}^{x_f} x^2 f(x)^2 \, dx ight] \]

Here, \( ho = r \) but it's variable, so we replace it by \( \int x \, dx \) instead:

\[ I_Y = \pi \int_{1}^{4} x \, dx \left[ rac{1}{4} \int_{1}^{4} e^{4x} \, dx + \int_{1}^{4} x^2 e^{2x} \, dx ight] \approx 13524736.28 \]

Hence, the moment of inertia of the plant around the y-axis is: \( I_Y = oxed{13524736.28} \).
